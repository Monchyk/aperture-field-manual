The Workshop Model

Five components. Together they turn "finish one thing" from a wish into a physical layout. Expand any station:

Exactly one hot bench — the active output flagship — at a time. Nothing else is hot. The other output life idles on maintenance touches. This extends the governor's R1 from the task layer up to the project layer.

Every new idea becomes a one-line inbox entry or a 25-minute spike — never a new bench. Novelty gets fed cheaply; nothing new gets promoted to a project. This is the release valve that makes the three-plan cap (R2) survivable instead of suffocating.

Claude finishes the grind tail — tests, docs, cleanup, wiring — gated to the hot bench. The apprentice never opens a second bench; it accelerates the one that's already open. This is where the whole gear system plugs in.

The day's gear is decided by the body, not the mood. Because the mood lies, and the body doesn't. See the Day Protocol below — it is the ground truth that the governor's battery pre-flight (R3) samples.

Shipping is the last creative act and gets marked — because the dopamine that finishing refuses to hand out, ceremony can teach. A weekly 10-minute ship-log to one trusted person manufactures the witnessed-finish reward the subject cannot generate alone.

The Three Lives

Two lives produce output. One recovers. Confusing them is how the engine seizes.

Programmer

OUTPUT. Gets the bench, the deep block, the finish ritual.

Musician

OUTPUT. Hard rule: the musician writes no code. A "music tool" is the programmer in disguise — file it as a ticket.

Gamer

RECOVERY. Protected, guilt-free. The fuel the other two burn — never a backlog.

MOST-VIOLATED PROTOCOL: "the musician writes no code." Building a synth plugin feels like making music. It is the programmer, wearing the musician's coat, opening a second bench. A real tool need becomes a timeboxed programmer ticket — never a musician detour.
